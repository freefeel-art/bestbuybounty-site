Why Omega-3 Form Matters More Than Dose

Most omega-3 supplements are sold on EPA+DHA milligram counts. The number on the label is real — but it misses the most important variable: what form are those fatty acids in, and how much actually absorbs?

Omega-3 fatty acids exist in two primary supplement forms: natural triglyceride (TG) and ethyl ester (EE). The natural triglyceride form is how omega-3s appear in whole fish. The ethyl ester form is a cheaper industrial processing product. Research published in peer-reviewed journals has shown that natural triglyceride omega-3 absorbs up to 70% more effectively than ethyl ester — meaning a lower-dose triglyceride supplement can deliver more bioavailable EPA+DHA than a higher-dose ethyl ester product.

Triglyceride vs Ethyl Ester: The Form That Determines Absorption

When fish oil is processed commercially, the natural triglyceride structure is typically broken apart. The fatty acids are concentrated as ethyl esters — a form that does not exist in nature and requires different enzymatic processing in the human digestive system.

The absorption difference matters in practice. A study published in the European Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that omega-3 in re-esterified triglyceride form was absorbed 124% more than ethyl ester form. A separate study in Prostaglandins, Leukotrienes and Essential Fatty Acids found triglyceride form superior to ethyl ester under fasted conditions particularly.

Purity: What Molecular Distillation Actually Removes

Deep-sea fish accumulate environmental contaminants in their tissue — mercury, lead, cadmium, PCBs, dioxins, and other persistent organic pollutants that concentrate up the food chain. This is a real concern, not a theoretical one: regulatory agencies in multiple countries issue fish consumption advisories specifically because of these contaminants.

Molecular distillation is the gold-standard purification process for fish oil. It works by heating the oil under high vacuum to temperatures where individual fatty acid molecules volatilise and separate from contaminants — which remain behind due to their different boiling points. The process is repeated to achieve pharmaceutical-grade purity.

What Purity Testing Covers

  • Heavy metals: Mercury, lead, cadmium, arsenic — all tested to levels below detectable limits
  • PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls): Industrial pollutants that accumulate in fish tissue
  • Dioxins and furans: Persistent environmental contaminants
  • Oxidation markers: Peroxide value and anisidine value — indicators of rancidity
  • Potency verification: EPA+DHA content confirmed against label claim

The Science Behind EPA and DHA

EPA (Eicosapentaenoic Acid)

EPA is the primary anti-inflammatory omega-3 fatty acid. It serves as a precursor to eicosanoids — signalling molecules that regulate inflammation, blood vessel function, and immune response. Research has consistently linked adequate EPA intake to cardiovascular health markers including triglyceride levels, blood pressure, and platelet aggregation. EPA is also involved in mood regulation and has been studied for its role in supporting mental wellbeing.

DHA (Docosahexaenoic Acid)

DHA is a structural fatty acid — it forms a major component of brain cell membranes and retinal tissue. The brain is approximately 60% fat, and DHA constitutes a significant proportion of that fat. Adequate DHA intake during development and across the lifespan is linked to cognitive function, visual acuity, and neurological health. DHA cannot be efficiently synthesised from plant-based omega-3 (ALA) — conversion rates in humans are typically below 5%.
